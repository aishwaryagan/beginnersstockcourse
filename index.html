How Much Should You Invest First?
Figuring out your investable income before buying your first share.
The golden rule: Never invest money you may need within the next 3–5 years. The market is volatile in the short term — you don't want to be forced to sell at a loss.

The 50/30/20 Starting Framework

A popular starting budget is the 50/30/20 rule: allocate 50% of take-home pay to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings & investments. Before investing, ensure you have:

  • An emergency fund covering 3–6 months of expenses (high-interest savings account)
  • All high-interest debt paid off (credit cards at 20%+ APR)
  • A basic budget that leaves a reliable monthly surplus

Choosing a Brokerage or Bank
Where you'll open an account and execute your trades.

What is a brokerage?

A brokerage is a licensed firm that executes buy/sell orders on your behalf on the stock exchange. There are two main types:

  • Full-service brokers — advisors who manage your portfolio for a fee (1–2% AUM/year). Best for large portfolios or hands-off investors.
  • Self-directed / discount brokers — you make your own trades, often commission-free. Best for beginners learning independently.

What to look for when choosing

  • Commission fees — aim for $0 or low per-trade costs
  • Account types supported — TFSA, RRSP, RESP, non-registered
  • Currency conversion fees — buying US stocks in Canada incurs FX costs (typically 1.5–2%)
  • User interface — especially important for beginners
  • CIPF protection — confirms your assets are insured if the broker fails
Canadian tip: The Canadian Investor Protection Fund (CIPF) protects up to $1M per account category if a member firm becomes insolvent. Always verify your broker is a CIPF member.

Sectors & Portfolio Diversification
Don't put all your eggs in one basket — spread risk intelligently.

What is diversification?

Diversification means spreading your investments across different asset types, sectors, and geographies so that a downturn in one area doesn't sink your entire portfolio. It's the closest thing to a free lunch in investing.

The easy diversification shortcut: Index ETFs

Instead of picking individual stocks, a single ETF gives you instant diversification:

  • XEQT / VEQT (Canada) — one-ticket all-equity ETFs, globally diversified, MER ~0.20%
  • SPY / VOO (USA) — tracks the S&P 500 (top 500 US companies)
  • VT — total world market in one fund
  • ZAG / VAB (Canada) — bond ETFs for the conservative portion of your portfolio
Age-based rule of thumb: 100 minus your age = % in stocks. A 30-year-old might hold 70% stocks, 30% bonds. Many modern investors use 110 or 120, given longer lifespans.

General rule: If your current marginal tax rate is lower than you expect in retirement, favour the TFSA. If you're in a high tax bracket now and expect less income in retirement, RRSP is more powerful.

Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) & Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)

The RRSP has two special withdrawal programs:

  • HBP: First-time homebuyers can withdraw up to $60,000 from their RRSP to purchase a home, repayable over 15 years.
  • LLP: Up to $10,000/year (max $20,000 total) can be withdrawn for full-time education, repayable over 10 years.
Over-contribution warning: Contributing over your TFSA or RRSP room results in a 1%/month penalty tax on the excess. Always verify your available room via CRA My Account.

Understanding Dividends
Getting paid just for holding shares — and how to evaluate dividend stocks.

What are dividends?

A dividend is a cash payment a company makes to shareholders from its profits, on a regular schedule (quarterly, monthly, or annually). Dividend stocks tend to be mature, stable businesses. Key metrics:

  • Dividend yield — Annual dividend ÷ current share price × 100. A 4% yield earns $4/year per $100 invested.
  • Payout ratio — % of earnings paid as dividends. Below 60% is healthy; above 80% may be unsustainable.
  • Ex-dividend date — You must own shares before this date to receive the next payment.
  • Dividend growth rate — Companies raising dividends annually (Dividend Aristocrats) are especially prized.

Automatic Re-investing (DRIP)
Let compounding do the heavy lifting while you sleep.

What is a DRIP?

A Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP) automatically uses your dividend payments to buy more shares — instead of paying cash to you. Over time, this creates a compounding snowball: more shares → more dividends → even more shares.

🔄 DRIP vs cash dividends over 10 years — $10,000 at 5% yield, 6% price growth

How to enable DRIP

  • At your brokerage: Most platforms (Questrade, TD, Fidelity) offer a DRIP toggle in account settings — usually free to enable.
  • Synthetic DRIP: Some brokers buy whole shares only; if dividend is less than one share price, cash accumulates.
  • Direct DRIP: Some companies offer DRIPs directly, sometimes at a 1–5% discount to market price.
The power of DRIP: Over 20 years, the same $10,000 investment with DRIP can produce 40–80% more wealth than taking dividends as cash — entirely from reinvested income compounding over time.

Beyond dividends: automatic contributions

Set up a pre-authorized contribution (PAC) — a scheduled automatic transfer from your bank to your investment account, which then auto-invests in a preset ETF. This removes emotion from the process and ensures you invest consistently regardless of market conditions.

When to Buy & When to Sell
Timing strategies, triggers, and the traps most beginners fall into.
"Time in the market beats timing the market." Decades of research show that most long-term returns are captured by just staying invested — not by trying to predict perfect entry and exit points.
📋 When to buy — common signals and strategies
📅
Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)Invest a fixed amount on a regular schedule regardless of price. Reduces volatility impact and eliminates market-timing anxiety.
📉
Market corrections (−10%) and bear markets (−20%)Historically, corrections are buying opportunities for long-term investors. Prices are "on sale." Requires emotional discipline not to panic sell instead.
💡
After new income or a windfallLump-sum investing often outperforms DCA historically (markets go up more often than down), but DCA reduces regret risk if timing is bad.
📊
When a stock hits your valuation targetFor individual stocks, buy when the price falls to or below your intrinsic value estimate — informed by P/E ratio, earnings growth, and comparable analysis.
🚪 When to sell — valid reasons and red flags
✅
RebalancingWhen one asset class grows far beyond your target allocation (e.g. stocks now 90% of a 70/30 portfolio), sell some to restore balance. Usually annual.
✅
Fundamental change in the businessThe reason you bought the stock no longer applies — competition destroyed the moat, management scandal, dividend was cut.
✅
Life circumstancesApproaching retirement or major purchase. Gradually shift to more conservative holdings over 3–5 years before the need.
⚠️
Panic selling during downturns — avoid!Selling because the market dropped is how most retail investors permanently destroy value. Downturns are temporary; selling locks in the loss.
⚠️
Selling great companies too earlySelling a strong compounding business to "lock in gains" often means missing years of further growth. Let your winners run.
Capital gains tax note (Canada): Selling investments for a profit outside a TFSA/RRSP triggers capital gains tax. Inside a TFSA, gains are always tax-free.

Bull & Bear Markets
How to read market sentiment — and keep your head when others don't.
🐂🐻 Explore market conditions
Bull Market — a sustained rise in prices of 20%+ from recent lows.

Characterized by rising stock prices, strong economic growth, low unemployment, high consumer confidence, and widespread optimism.

How to invest: Stay invested, continue DCA contributions, consider a slight increase in equity allocation. Don't overleverage or FOMO into speculative assets.
Bear Market — a 20%+ decline from recent highs sustained over 2+ months.

Characterized by falling prices, economic contraction or recession fear, rising unemployment, and widespread pessimism.

How to invest: This is often the best time to buy. Continue DCA — you're buying more shares at lower prices. Don't sell solid long-term holdings.

Other market conditions you'll hear about

  • Correction — A 10–20% decline from recent highs. Common and healthy; the S&P 500 experiences a correction roughly every 1–2 years.
  • Market rally — A strong, quick upswing in prices, often after a period of selling.
  • Dead cat bounce — A temporary recovery in a declining market, followed by further drops.
  • Secular bull/bear market — A very long-term trend (decade+) vs a cyclical bull/bear (months to a few years).

Gauging the Market & Staying Informed
The key indicators, tools, and sources every investor should know.

Key market indicators

  • Major indices — S&P 500, TSX Composite, NASDAQ, Dow Jones. These are the "score" of the overall market.
  • VIX (Volatility Index) — The "fear gauge." Above 30 signals high uncertainty/panic; below 15 signals calm.
  • P/E Ratio (Shiller CAPE) — Compares stock prices to earnings. CAPE above 30 may indicate historical overvaluation.
  • Yield curve — When short-term bond yields exceed long-term yields (inversion), it has historically preceded recessions by 12–18 months.
  • CPI / Inflation data — High inflation erodes purchasing power and often prompts rate hikes, which can compress stock valuations.
  • Interest rates (Bank of Canada / Fed) — Rate hike cycles tend to pressure growth stocks; rate cut cycles often boost markets.

READING
Beware of financial media noise. Daily market commentary is mostly noise. Check your portfolio quarterly, not daily. Short-term price moves rarely contain actionable information for a long-term investor.

Building your investing routine

  • Monthly: Make your regular contribution, enable DRIP
  • Quarterly: Review portfolio allocation, read earnings for holdings that matter to you
  • Annually: Rebalance if needed, maximize TFSA/RRSP contributions, review your investment plan
  • As-needed: Respond to genuine fundamental changes in your holdings, not to short-term price swings
